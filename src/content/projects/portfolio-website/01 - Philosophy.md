# 01 — Philosophy

> The "why" of this site. Practical, not literary. Rewritten 2026-05-17 after user feedback that the first draft over-borrowed the Legendary UI/UX manifesto's posture.

## The brief, in one line

A portfolio site that's actually impressive and actually cool — showing school, research, projects, and the obsessive traceability behind them — built with enough aesthetic discipline that the reader notices the work, not the chrome.

## What this site has to do

1. **Show who Rafan is.** CS @ SJSU. Reed Systems Group DDD research. The stated goal of meaningful applications of CS.
2. **Show what he's built.** Small projects with rigorous documentation, screenshots, GitHub/download links.
3. **Show how he works.** "Field notes" affordance — cleaned Obsidian-style traceability he can publish without leaking the personal stuff.
4. **Be a good website.** Smooth on Mac and phone. Free hosting. Editable without code changes for the high-frequency stuff. Real photo of him fading in when he drops one in.

## Where the aesthetic comes from

The visual language inherits from his [[Legendary UI-UX/Legendary UI-UX|Legendary UI/UX]] project — warm-paper-on-ink palette, system serif type, subtle motion, restraint over volume. That aesthetic already produced something he liked, so it's the starting point. Not because the Legendary manifesto is sacred (much of that project's *prose* was generated by an earlier AI pass and shouldn't be treated as Rafan's authoritative voice), but because the *visual taste* there is sharp and saves us a "design from zero" pass.

Carrying over from Legendary: inertial typography, synesthesia coloring (scoped to one paragraph, not a whole tab), cursor companion, the color discipline. Dropping: the audio drone, mnemonic margin, four-room manifesto structure, aurora, constellation — those argued things specific to that piece and don't have a job here.

Adding new: the manila-folder gallery, the recursive-tree backdrop (from his Manim_Wallpaper project), the portrait-with-fade hero.

## Where the content comes from

**The user writes the words.** Build tooling helps with structure and place-holding, but no copy on the live site is written *as* Rafan unless he wrote it. Placeholder prose in components is marked obvious-placeholder (`[YOUR BIO HERE — ~80 words]` style, or visible Lorem) so he sees immediately where to fill in. Bio paragraph, DDD story, project stories, "on the trail" prose — all his.

The only fixed text that came from him directly is the tagline (verbatim, from his original message; see [[Author Profile]]). Everything else is his to write.

## What this site is *not*

- Not a manifesto.
- Not a resume in HTML — the resume PDF is linked, not duplicated.
- Not loud — no autoplay video, no music, no flashy intro.
- Not done in one pass. The first ship is the start.

## Six principles (kept short)

1. **The medium is the argument where it can be.** The "on recursion" section runs a live recursive tree because the section is about a person studying recursion. Where the page can run a thing instead of describing it, it runs the thing. (One genuine borrow from Legendary; this principle does real work here.)
2. **Nothing is loud.** Low-amplitude motion, restrained palette, no shouting type.
3. **Curated openness.** Personal vault stays private. Site shows cleaned, hand-curated copies. [[04 - Content Pipeline]] is documented and reviewable.
4. **Modular and reviewable.** Code passes senior-eng scrutiny. Every primitive self-contained. Every non-trivial decision has an ADR. ([[Working Agreements]].)
5. **Built to be picked up.** User may continue from a different tool, account, or agent. The Obsidian docs are the durable artifact, not memory.
6. **His voice, his words.** Placeholder stays placeholder until he replaces it.

## How to apply this in practice

- When writing a new component, if it needs body copy, write `[PLACEHOLDER — what should go here, target length]` and stop.
- When in doubt about aesthetic tone, choose restraint. Legendary's discipline is the floor; "impressive and cool" is the ceiling.
- When a decision has reasonable alternatives, write an ADR. Don't decide in your head.
- When updating something in memory, update the corresponding Obsidian doc in the same turn.
